KEY FINDINGS:

1. STRONG LINEAR RELATIONSHIP CONFIRMED
Our analysis confirms a robust linear relationship between time and global temperature rise, evidenced by the high R² values in both proposed models. The statistical significance of the regression coefficients (p < 0.001) underscores that temperature increase is not a random fluctuation but a predictable consequence of time-dependent factors, validating the fundamental premise of the study.

2. WARMING ACCELERATION DETECTED
Beyond simple linear growth, the quadratic model reveals a critical positive acceleration (β₂ = 0.000338). The warming rate has escalated dramatically, from a relatively slow 0.006°C/year in 1961 to a rapid 0.047°C/year by 2022. This eightfold increase indicates that climate change is intensifying, with each passing decade adding approximately 0.4°C to the warming rate.

3. PARIS AGREEMENT TRAJECTORY
Current trends point to a concerning overshoot of international climate goals. The model projects a warming of 1.93°C by 2030, exceeding the Paris Agreement's aspirational 1.5°C threshold by nearly 0.43°C. This trajectory highlights the insufficiency of current passive trends and the urgent need for aggressive intervention to alter the course.

4. MODEL RELIABILITY
Diagnostic tests confirm the reliability of these findings. Residuals follows a normal distribution without significant patterns of heteroscedasticity, suggesting the model captures the underlying trend effectively. However, it is crucial to note that uncertainty increases exponentially with extrapolation; while the 2030 projection is robust, predictions further into the future rely heavily on the assumption of varied stability.


LIMITATIONS:

1. STATISTICAL LIMITATIONS:
While the aggregate metrics are robust, the country-level aggregation inevitably masks significant regional heterogeneity. Furthermore, representing complex climate dynamics with simple polynomial models is a necessary simplification; these models extrapolate history without accounting for potential regime shifts caused by new policies or sudden natural events, leading to widening confidence intervals beyond the 2030 horizon.

2. MODEL ASSUMPTIONS:
The projections rely on the assumption that current emission trajectories will continue unchanged. Crucially, they do not dynamically model feedback loops (such as permafrost thawing or albedo loss) or strictly enforce the impact of future policy interventions like the Paris Agreement. They differ from mechanistic climate models (CMIP6) by being purely data-driven extrapolations of the past.

3. DATA LIMITATIONS:
The analysis is constrained by the 62-year historical record (1961-2022), which is relatively short in climatological terms. Additionally, focusing solely on temperature change at the national level excludes other critical variables like precipitation patterns and extreme weather events, which are essential for a holistic risk assessment.

4. SCOPE:
This report establishes a baseline scenario intended for business planning and risk assessment. It should be viewed as a foundational trend analysis rather than a comprehensive climate simulation. For high-stakes decisions, these findings should be integrated with detailed scenario analyses (e.g., RCP 2.6, 4.5, 8.5) and broader scientific reports.

Simultaneously, adaptation strategies must be developed for a realistic worst-case scenario of 2°C warming, ensuring organizational climate literacy and integrating these scenarios into the core strategic planning process.

4. MONITORING & UPDATING:
Climate risk management requires a dynamic, adaptive approach. Models should be updated annually with the latest data to monitor deviations from projected trajectories. Strategies must remain flexible, adjusting to emerging climate science and benchmarking performance against authoritative assessments like the IPCC reports to ensure continued relevance and safety.
